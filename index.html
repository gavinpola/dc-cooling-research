Three-Thesis Analysis of Data Center Cooling Technologies

This report evaluates three distinct approaches to data center cooling for AI infrastructure: two-phase direct-to-chip liquid cooling, high ambient temperature tolerance, and passive radiative cooling to space. Our analysis concludes that liquid cooling technology—particularly two-phase direct-to-chip—represents the primary investable thesis.

Thesis A: Two-Phase Direct-to-Chip Cooling

Two-phase direct-to-chip liquid cooling using dielectric fluids or refrigerants will become the dominant cooling approach for high-density AI data centers.

Technical Foundation

  • Heat transfer coefficients of 10,000-23,200 W/m²K (50-100x improvement over air cooling)[12][13]
  • Capable of managing rack densities of 150-500kW (compared to ~40kW ceiling for air cooling)[1]
  • NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 systems at 120kW continuous load require liquid cooling by specification[14]
  • Vertiv currently holds greater than 70% market share for GB200 rack cooling systems[15]

Bull Case

  • Physics advantage through latent heat transfer is permanent[16]
  • Active deployment by Meta, Microsoft, Google, and AWS[2]
  • 41% CAPEX savings versus air cooling for 10MW facilities[17]
  • 35% OpEx savings versus single-phase liquid alternatives[18]
  • Non-PFAS alternatives (HFO refrigerants) address regulatory concerns[19]

Bear Case

  • PFAS regulatory risk for fluorocarbon-based fluids[20]
  • Single-phase microchannel technology closing performance gap[21]
  • Higher operational complexity versus single-phase systems
  • Supply chain concentration presents vendor risk
  • May remain specialized rather than achieving true dominance

Thesis B: High Ambient Temperature Tolerance

High ambient temperature tolerance (35-45°C+) represents an economically rational approach to data center cooling, enabling reduction or elimination of mechanical cooling through operational optimization.

Critical Assessment

  • ASHRAE H1 class specifications for AI equipment require 18-25°C—more restrictive, not less[22]
  • This represents an operational practice rather than an investable technology
  • Leading hyperscalers have already adopted these practices; limited growth opportunity remains[3]
  • Does not address the fundamental density challenge; liquid cooling remains necessary for 120kW+ racks

Bull Case

  • Google has operated without chillers since 2008 (Belgium facility)[23]
  • Meta Prineville operates successfully at 110°F outdoor temperatures[24]
  • 30-50% cooling energy reduction achievable in suitable climates[25]
  • Free cooling available 90%+ of year in Nordic regions[26]

Bear Case

  • AI equipment specifications trend toward cooler requirements (H1 class)[22]
  • Strategy fails in humid climates (Singapore, Southeast Asia)[27]
  • Increased fan power consumption partially offsets temperature savings
  • No direct investment thesis—operational practice only

Thesis C: Space-Based Passive Radiative Cooling

Passive radiative cooling to space could theoretically reduce or eliminate active mechanical cooling by exploiting the atmospheric transparency window (8-13 micrometers).[28]

Fundamental Limitations

  • Scale mismatch: Technology delivers approximately 100 W/m² versus data center requirements of 100+ kW per rack (500-5,000x gap)[4][29]
  • Land requirements: A single 100kW rack would require 1,000 m² of radiative panels
  • Geographic constraints: Performance degrades significantly in humid climates where major data center markets are located[30]
  • Physics ceiling: Approximately 150 W/m² represents the theoretical maximum[31]
  • Market positioning: SkyCool Systems operates in building HVAC augmentation, not data center cooling[32]

Bull Case

  • Zero electricity and zero water consumption in operation
  • SkyCool demonstrates 10-40% HVAC efficiency improvements[32]
  • Potential application for edge computing supplementation
  • Orbital computing development driving R&D investment[33]

Bear Case

  • Cannot bridge the 500-5,000x scale gap[4]
  • Physics limits represent hard constraints, not engineering problems
  • Hong Kong field tests showed no daytime cooling effect[30]
  • Liquid cooling has already solved the core problem

Liquid Cooling Modality Competition: What Wins for Feynman (2028)?

The "liquid vs air" debate is settled—liquid wins. The real question is: which liquid cooling modality dominates as TDP climbs toward 4-6kW per module?

The TDP Trajectory

Architecture Year TDP (Module) Cooling Required
Hopper (H100) 2022 700W[34] Air or liquid
Blackwell (B200) 2024 1,200W[9] Liquid mandatory
Rubin (R100) 2026 2,000-2,300W[35] Advanced liquid
Feynman 2028 4,400-6,000W[36][37] Embedded/microfluidic

Key Insight

Feynman introduces 3D logic die stacking—multiple GPU dies stacked on top of each other.[38] This isn't just higher TDP; it's a fundamental thermal architecture challenge that traditional cold plates cannot solve.

The Breakthrough: TSMC Direct-to-Silicon Cooling

TSMC demonstrated direct-to-silicon liquid cooling at ECTC 2025, with micropillar arrays etched directly into the silicon backside.[41] This achieves:

  • Zero TIM: Coolant contacts silicon directly (no thermal interface material)
  • 0.055 °C/W thermal resistance—15% better than lidded solutions[41]
  • 2.6kW+ demonstrated with temperature delta under 63°C
  • Backside cooling—essential for 3D-stacked Feynman architecture

Startup Founder's Guide

Assessment for New Entrants

Should you start a liquid cooling company in 2026? Likely not, unless you possess a specific unfair advantage. The market is consolidating, category leaders are established[15], and strategic acquirers are actively purchasing startups at compressed multiples.[47]

Market Entry Challenges

Market Reality Impact on New Entrants
Vertiv holds 70%+ GB200 market share[15] Hyperscalers have established vendor relationships
Four major acquisitions in 18 months Strategic consolidation reduces exit options and valuations
18-24 month qualification cycles[48] Extended sales cycles require significant runway
Hardware manufacturing scale requirements Capital-intensive competition on cost basis
Technology has been validated No "10x improvement" breakthrough opportunities available

Opportunity Assessment by Tier

Non-PFAS Coolant Chemistry
Tier 1 · Defensible

Two-phase cooling faces PFAS regulatory risk.[20] Developer of a compliant drop-in replacement fluid captures significant value. Requires chemistry moat and benefits from regulatory tailwind.

Target founders: Chemistry PhDs, spinouts from Honeywell or Chemours

Thermal Management Software
Tier 1 · Defensible

CDUs are hardware; optimization is software. Dynamic cooling optimization delivers 10-20% energy savings. Software margins significantly exceed hardware margins. Data moat compounds over time.

Target founders: ML engineers with data center operations experience

Emerging Market Champion
Tier 1 · Defensible

India, Southeast Asia, and Middle East scaling capacity rapidly. Global players have limited local presence. Relationships and local manufacturing create sustainable advantage.

Target founders: Based in target geography with existing DC operator relationships

Edge Computing Cooling
Tier 2 · Niche

Edge deployments (5-50kW) have distinct requirements: modular, self-contained, ruggedized. Higher margins per unit, different sales channels. TAM limitation of approximately $500M-1B may constrain venture outcomes.

Defense and Aerospace
Tier 2 · Niche

Government sector has specific requirements (ITAR compliance, security clearances). High margins, sticky relationships, less price-sensitive procurement. Extended sales cycles and small customer base present limitations.

Generic Direct-to-Chip CDUs
Avoid

Vertiv, CoolIT, and Motivair have established dominant positions.[15] Hyperscalers will not switch vendors for marginal improvements. Manufacturing scale economics favor incumbents. "Better engineering" is not sufficient differentiation.

Most data center cooling exits fall in the $50-200M range. Outcomes exceeding $1B are uncommon in hardware businesses. Exits occur at strategic value (portfolio gap completion) rather than revenue multiples.

Founder Decision Framework

Consider Starting If

  • You have a specific technical breakthrough (not incremental improvement)
  • You have existing hyperscaler or DC operator relationships
  • You are targeting an underserved niche
  • You are building software or services (not pure hardware)
  • You accept $50-200M exit outcomes
  • You have 5-7 year time horizon

Do Not Start If

  • Your plan is "build a better CDU"
  • You are a first-time hardware entrepreneur
  • You require venture-scale ($1B+) outcomes
  • You lack data center industry experience
  • You would compete directly with Vertiv or CoolIT
  • Your differentiation is "better engineering"

Alternative Paths

  • Join a scaling startup: ZutaCore and Accelsius are actively hiring. Obtain equity exposure while learning the market with lower personal risk.
  • Join a strategic acquirer: Vertiv, Schneider Electric, and Eaton are building teams. Develop customer relationships and industry expertise before founding a company.
  • Start an adjacent business: Data center facility design consulting, thermal testing and qualification services, or maintenance service provision. Lower risk with faster path to revenue.

Summary Assessment

The liquid cooling market represents a significant and growing opportunity. However, for a new startup in 2026, timing presents challenges. The technology innovation phase is concluding. Market consolidation is accelerating. Customer relationships are established. The market opportunity is substantial; the startup entry opportunity is more constrained.
