Thesis A: Two-Phase Direct-to-Chip Cooling

The Core Thesis

Two-phase direct-to-chip liquid cooling using dielectric fluids or refrigerants will become the dominant cooling approach for high-density AI data centers.

Why It Works

  • Heat transfer coefficients of 10,000-23,200 W/m2K (50-100x air cooling)
  • Handles rack densities of 150-500kW (vs 40kW air ceiling)
  • NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 at 120kW requires liquid cooling
  • Vertiv holds 70%+ market share for GB200 cooling

Bull Case Highlights

  • Physics advantage is permanent - latent heat transfer
  • Hyperscalers deploying at scale (Meta, Microsoft, Google, AWS)
  • 41% CAPEX savings vs air cooling for 10MW DC
  • 35% OpEx savings vs single-phase liquid
  • Non-PFAS alternatives exist (HFO refrigerants)

Bear Case Highlights

  • PFAS regulatory risk for fluorocarbon fluids
  • Single-phase microchannels closing performance gap
  • Operational complexity vs single-phase
  • Supply chain concentration risk
  • May become niche, not dominant

Thesis B: High Ambient Temperature Tolerance

The Core Thesis

High ambient temperature tolerance (35-45C+) represents the most economically rational approach to data center cooling, enabling elimination of mechanical cooling through architectural and operational optimization.

The Reality Check

  • ASHRAE H1 class (for AI equipment) requires 18-25C - tighter, not looser
  • This is an operational practice, not a technology you can invest in
  • Hyperscalers already do this - no growth opportunity
  • Doesn't solve density problem (still need liquid cooling for 120kW+ racks)

Bull Case Highlights

  • Google chiller-less since 2008 (Belgium)
  • Meta Prineville operates at 110F outdoor temps
  • 30-50% cooling energy reduction possible
  • Free cooling works 90%+ of year in Nordic climates

Bear Case Highlights

  • AI chips need COOLER temps, not warmer (H1 class)
  • Fails in humid climates (Singapore, Southeast Asia)
  • Fan power increases offset temperature savings
  • Not investable - it's an operating practice

Thesis C: Space-Based Passive Radiative Cooling

The Core Thesis

Passive radiative cooling to space could dramatically reduce or eliminate active mechanical cooling in data centers by exploiting the atmospheric transparency window (8-13 micrometers).

Why It Fails

  • Scale mismatch: 100 W/m2 capacity vs 100 kW/rack requirement (500-5,000x gap)
  • A single 100kW rack would need 1,000 m2 of panels (quarter acre)
  • Geographic constraints: Fails in humid climates where major DC markets are
  • Physics ceiling: ~150 W/m2 is the theoretical maximum
  • Building HVAC technology: SkyCool is an HVAC company, not DC cooling

Bull Case Highlights

  • Zero electricity, zero water operation
  • SkyCool achieves 10-40% HVAC efficiency gains
  • Could supplement edge computing cooling
  • Orbital computing driving R&D investment

Bear Case Highlights

  • Cannot bridge 500-5,000x scale gap
  • Physics limits are hard caps, not engineering problems
  • Hong Kong tests: NO daytime cooling effect
  • Liquid cooling already solved the problem
  • A $5,000 mini-split is the competitor for edge
